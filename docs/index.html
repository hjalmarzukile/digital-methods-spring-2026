Weeks 16–22 Tuesdays and Thursdays Lecture room: CSS 2-0-63

Overview

What the course is trying to teach

Course description

Digital methods focus on media-specific digital traces and on combining qualitative and quantitative inquiry in ways that fit how online data is actually generated. Students work with exploratory network analysis, digital ethnography, and content analysis while building their own integrated mapping of a public issue.

Project format

Teaching combines lectures, method exercises, group project work, and near-weekly supervision. The course is organized around a group project that becomes the basis for a written assignment and oral exam.

3–4 students per exam group
20–25 pages in the written assignment
6 supervision sessions per group

Lecture Map

What each lecture is trying to do

These summaries are based on the lecture content notes and critiques, not the slide decks.

Lecture 1

Mixed digital methods as the course frame

The course starts with the final project, then defines digital, mixed, and mixed digital methods across data, methods, and analysis.

  • Strong move: start with the exam and the overall project goal
  • Core tension: what counts as “methods of the medium”
  • Main revision need: reduce repeated definitional slides

Lecture 2

Questions and research design

Students move from topic to casing to research question and then to an initial mixed-method design.

  • Question typologies are concrete and reusable
  • Casing links empirical topics to theoretical stakes
  • Design logic should work as the course’s master template

Lecture 3

Finding and judging datasites

Students scout digital environments, evaluate them, and begin an immersion journal.

  • Useful process: simplification, search, scouting, evaluation, selection
  • Important concern: ethics and representation
  • Main risk: too much reading for one week

Lecture 4

Data design and data collection

The focus shifts from finding sites to deciding what to collect, how to sample, and how to integrate data sources.

  • Strong on data-integration and sampling typologies
  • Needs more explicit tool guidance for current APIs and scraping
  • Best used as a bridge from design to hands-on collection

Lecture 5

Discovery and open coding

Students learn how exploratory computational methods and qualitative reading can work together during discovery.

  • Computer-assisted, not computer-led, is the key framing
  • Open coding is the human grounding step
  • Needs a clearer conceptual intro to text-as-data workflows

Lecture 6

Focused coding and classification

The course moves from exploratory interpretation to codebooks, focused coding, and text classification.

  • Excellent worked example from refugee solidarity research
  • Important bridge from qualitative insight to measurable variables
  • Needs clearer comparison of classifier options and trade-offs

Lecture 7

Quantitative text analysis

This is the least developed lecture in the current material, but it should cover validation and measurement.

  • Classifier validation: precision, recall, F1, confusion matrix
  • From labels to distributions, trends, and comparisons
  • Needs a dedicated slide deck and stronger methods examples

Lecture 8

Mixed analysis design and reporting

The final teaching sequence asks what role each analysis plays in the overall project and how quality should be judged.

  • Best used as a design clinic for real student projects
  • Brings together confirmatory and complementary logics
  • Connects analysis design to reporting quality and exam preparation

Outro

Closing synthesis and exam guidance

The closing session should synthesize methodological lessons, position the course in a wider methods landscape, and prepare students for the oral exam.

  • Strong place to discuss what the course did not cover
  • Important opportunity for ethics of representation
  • Should consolidate rather than repeat earlier exam content
