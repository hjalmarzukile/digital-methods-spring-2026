## Session 2 — Thursday 16/4
**Topic: Research question and mixed research designs**
*(Following Lecture 2)*

### Structured exercise (1 hour)

**Reverse-engineering a published mixed methods study (60 min)**

This exercise trains the skill of reading a published study analytically — understanding not just what was found, but *how it was designed*.

**Preparation:** The TA assigns Carlsen, Gårdhus & Toubøl (2023) "Ukrainian refugee solidarity mobilization online" (from the reading list) as the shared text for this session.

**Part 1 — Individual reading (15 min)**
Read the methods section and the analysis design of the paper. Note down:
- What is the research question?
- What data types are used?
- How are qualitative and quantitative analyses combined?

**Part 2 — Group mapping (25 min)**
As a group, map the study using the course framework:
1. Draw the analysis design as a simple diagram. Is it sequential, parallel, or nested?
2. Which type of data integration is used: integrated, nested/hierarchical, or transformative?
3. What does the qualitative sub-study contribute that the quantitative analysis alone could not?
4. If you were to critique the design, what would you say?

**Part 3 — Discussion (20 min)**
Full class discussion: What design decisions can we learn from for our own projects? What does "mixing" actually buy you in this study?

**→ 11:00–13:00: Project work**
Formulate a tentative research question for your group project. Sketch a preliminary mixed method design: What data will you need? What will the qualitative component do? What will the quantitative component do? Why combine them?

## Session 3 — Tuesday 21/4
**Topic: Finding, evaluating and selecting digital data sources**
*(Following Lecture 3)*

### Structured exercise (1 hour)

**Guided scouting: applying the investigation process and the reality checklist (60 min)**

The purpose is to practice the full scouting workflow from lecture — from search terms to evaluated datasites — on a shared example before applying it to your own projects.

**Shared topic for the exercise:** Online communities discussing *preparedness / prepping* (as used in the lecture example).

**Part 1 — Simplification and search (15 min)**
As a group, devise a list of search terms. Use the three routes from lecture:
- Topical words → sites → actors
- Topical actors → words → sites
- Topical platforms → words → actors

Identify 3–4 potential datasites from your search.

**Part 2 — Evaluate using the reality checklist (25 min)**
Apply Venturini et al.'s (2018) "reality checklist" criteria to two of your candidate datasites. For each datasite, assess:
- *Representativity*: who is actually present here? Who is absent?
- *Reactivity*: does the presence of researchers or data collection affect the data?
- *Traces vs. actions*: are you capturing what people do, or only traces of what they do?
- *Ethical access*: is data ethically accessible? (Apply Kozinets's ethics flowchart)

**Part 3 — Selection and justification (20 min)**
Which datasite would you select and why? Each group presents their selection and one key reason (3 minutes). The TA facilitates brief discussion.

**→ 11:00–13:00: Project work**
Apply the same scouting process to your own project topic. Scout for at least two candidate datasites. Begin writing up your datasite descriptions in your immersion journal. Work toward Milestone 1 (due 24/4).

## Session 4 — Thursday 23/4
**Supervision 1: Find, evaluate and select digital data sources**

### Structured exercise (1 hour)

**Peer datasite review (60 min)**

Each group presents their shortlisted datasites and receives structured feedback before finalizing their choice for Milestone 1.

**Format:**
- Each group gets 10 minutes: 5 minutes presentation + 5 minutes peer feedback.
- Presentation must cover: (1) the datasite, (2) why it fits the research question, (3) how it was evaluated using quality criteria, (4) any ethical concerns identified.
- Peer feedback should be structured around: Is the justification convincing? What are the main risks with this datasite? Is there a better alternative?

**TA role:** Take notes on common challenges across groups. In the final 10 minutes, summarize the key points for the whole class.

**→ 11:00–13:00: Project work**
Finalize your datasite selection. Complete your immersion journal entries on the datasites. Write up your Milestone 1 submission (due Friday 24/4 at 23:59).

## Session 5 — Tuesday 28/4
**Topic: Data design and data collection**
*(Following Lecture 4)*

### Structured exercise (1 hour)

**Data integration types and sampling strategy workshop (60 min)**

**Part 1 — Data integration exercise (20 min)**
The TA presents three short research scenarios. For each, groups discuss and decide which data integration type applies (integrated, nested/hierarchical, transformative) and explain their reasoning.

*Scenario A:* A researcher collects Instagram posts from activists *and* conducts interviews with the same activists, then compares how they present the cause publicly vs. privately.

*Scenario B:* A researcher studies Facebook groups about housing rights. She qualitatively analyses 5 groups to identify key frames, then uses those frames as labels to classify 50,000 posts from the same groups.

*Scenario C:* A researcher studies Twitter accounts of climate NGOs and collects both the text of their tweets and the number of retweets, to see if certain discursive frames generate more engagement.

**Part 2 — Sampling design (25 min)**
Based on the Rafail (2018) reading on nonprobability sampling for Twitter: which sampling strategy is appropriate for your project's main data source? Groups sketch their sampling approach:
- What is the population you are sampling from?
- What type of sampling will you use (keyword-based, snowball, purposive, etc.)?
- What are the key limitations of your approach?

**Part 3 — Brief feedback round (15 min)**
Groups share their sampling strategies. TA provides feedback on feasibility and validity.

**→ 11:00–13:00: Project work**
Begin designing your data collection. Write up your data collection plan. Test the feasibility of your collection approach. Work toward Milestone 2 (due 1/5).

## Session 6 — Thursday 30/4
**Supervision 2: Data design and data collection**

### Structured exercise (1 hour)

**Data collection pipeline walkthrough (60 min)**

The goal of this session is to get everyone's data collection actually working.

**Part 1 — Tool check and setup (20 min)**
Each group demonstrates their current data collection setup to the TA. This could be:
- A script or API call that retrieves data from their chosen platform
- A manual data collection protocol they are following
- A download procedure from a data archive or platform export

The TA circulates and helps troubleshoot any technical issues.

**Part 2 — Data quality first look (25 min)**
Groups open their collected data (even if preliminary) and do a first quality check:
- How many items do you have so far?
- Are there obvious problems (duplicates, missing fields, wrong time period)?
- Does the data actually represent what you thought it would?
- Does the data fit your research question? Does it prompt any revisions?

**Part 3 — Revised plan (15 min)**
Each group states: what will they collect this week, how much, and by when? Commit to a concrete data collection target for Milestone 2.

**→ 11:00–13:00: Project work**
Data collection. The full remaining time is for active data collection with TA support available.

## Session 7 — Tuesday 5/5
**Topic: Computer assisted discovery and open coding**
*(Following Lecture 5)*

### Structured exercise (1 hour)

**Collective open coding session on a shared corpus (60 min)**

This session gives students hands-on experience with open coding before applying it to their own data. The TA prepares a shared mini-corpus in advance (15–20 social media posts on a public topic, e.g. posts from a public Facebook group or Reddit thread on a newsworthy issue).

**Part 1 — Individual coding (20 min)**
Each student codes the shared corpus independently. Coding instructions:
- Read through all posts first.
- Go back and assign short labels (codes) to anything that seems analytically interesting — themes, framings, emotional tones, rhetorical moves, etc.
- Write brief memos on any surprises or puzzles.
- Aim for 3–8 codes total.

**Part 2 — Group comparison (25 min)**
Groups compare codes:
- What codes did you share? What does overlap suggest?
- Where did you diverge? What does divergence reveal about the data and about interpretation?
- Which codes feel most analytically promising, and why?
- Are any codes candidates for focused analysis?

**Part 3 — Computational discovery connection (15 min)**
The TA shows briefly: if we ran a word frequency or word2vec expansion on this corpus using the codes as seed words, what might we find? This bridges open coding to the computational discovery workflow from the lecture.

**→ 11:00–13:00: Project work**
Begin open coding your own project data. Set up the computational discovery workflow on your own corpus (keyword search, word frequency, or word2vec as relevant). Continue data collection if needed.

## Session 8 — Thursday 7/5
**Supervision 3: Computer assisted discovery and open coding**

### Structured exercise (1 hour)

**Emerging themes share-out and abductive reflection (60 min)**

**Part 1 — What have you found? (30 min)**
Each group shares (3–5 minutes):
- 3 of their most interesting open codes, with one example text for each.
- One thing that surprised them in the data.
- One tension or puzzle they have not yet resolved.

**Part 2 — Abductive discussion (20 min)**
Drawing on Timmermans & Tavory's concept of abductive analysis: for each group's surprise or puzzle, the class discusses — *what would have to be true about the social world for this pattern to exist?* This is a way to move from data observations toward theoretical insight.

**Part 3 — Focus narrowing (10 min)**
Each group names one or two codes or themes they plan to pursue into focused coding. The TA notes these on the board to track across groups.

**→ 11:00–13:00: Project work**
Continue open coding and computer-assisted discovery. Begin transitioning from open to focused coding. Continue or finalize data collection.

## Session 9 — Tuesday 12/5
**Topic: Computer assisted focused coding and text classification**
*(Following Lecture 6)*

### Structured exercise (1 hour)

**Codebook development workshop (60 min)**

The goal is to move from open codes to a structured codebook — the foundation for both qualitative write-up and text classification.

**Part 1 — Codebook anatomy (10 min)**
The TA walks through the structure of a well-formed codebook category (based on MacQueen et al. 1998):
- Category name and short label
- Full definition
- When to use (inclusion criteria)
- When NOT to use (exclusion criteria)
- 2–3 example texts

**Part 2 — Codebook drafting (30 min)**
Each group selects 2–3 of their focused codes and drafts full codebook entries for them. Groups work at their own pace, but the TA circulates to push on definition precision: "Would two independent coders apply this code the same way?"

**Part 3 — Cross-group review (20 min)**
Groups swap codebook drafts with another group. Reviewers read each category and ask: Is the definition clear? Could we apply it without ambiguity? Give one suggestion per category.

**→ 11:00–13:00: Project work**
Revise codebook based on feedback. Continue focused coding of your own data. Begin planning your text classifier: will you use a dictionary, LLM prompting (Törnberg 2024), or supervised ML?

## Session 10 — Thursday 14/5
**Supervision 4: Computer assisted focused coding and text classification**

### Structured exercise (1 hour)

**Intercoder reliability mini-exercise (60 min)**

The goal is to test and sharpen codebook definitions through disagreement analysis.

**Part 1 — Cross-coding (25 min)**
Each group selects 10 texts from their own data. Two members of the group each code those 10 texts independently using the draft codebook — without discussing.

**Part 2 — Disagreement analysis (25 min)**
Compare codes for each of the 10 texts:
- Where did you agree? What makes those cases clear?
- Where did you disagree? Is the disagreement due to (a) an ambiguous definition, (b) a genuinely difficult case, or (c) a case the codebook did not anticipate?
- Revise the category definition to address each type of disagreement.

**Part 3 — Validation planning (10 min)**
For groups planning to use automated classification (LLM or supervised ML): how will you build your test set? How many items? Who will label them? Commit to a labelling plan.

**→ 11:00–13:00: Project work**
Continue focused coding. Build or refine text classifier. Work toward Milestone 3 (due 15/5 at 23:59). Receive TA feedback on Milestone 2.

## Session 11 — Tuesday 19/5
**Topic: Text classification and quantitative text analysis**
*(Following Lecture 7)*

### Structured exercise (1 hour)

**Unpacking a published classification study (60 min)**

Using one of the course readings as a case study, this session trains students to read quantitative text analysis critically — understanding how classification decisions shape results.

**Assigned text for this session:** Nelson (2021) "Cycles of Conflict, a Century of Continuity" *or* Greve et al. (2022) "Online Conspiracy Groups" (TA chooses based on group projects).

**Part 1 — Methods reading (15 min)**
Re-read the methods and results sections of the assigned paper with a specific focus:
- What text classification method did they use?
- How did they validate it?
- How is the classifier output used in the quantitative analysis?
- What assumptions does the approach rest on?

**Part 2 — Group critique (25 min)**
Groups discuss:
1. Could you replicate this classification approach? What would you need?
2. What are the main threats to validity in their approach?
3. How does their approach compare to what you are planning for your own project?

**Part 3 — Apply to own project (20 min)**
Each group sketches their own classification + quantitative analysis plan:
- What is the quantitative measure you want to produce (e.g. a proportion, a trend over time, a comparison across groups)?
- What does your classifier need to do to produce that measure?
- How will you validate it?

**→ 11:00–13:00: Project work**
Work on quantitative text analysis. Label test set for evaluating your classifier. Receive TA feedback on Milestone 3.

## Session 12 — Thursday 21/5
**Supervision 5: Text classification and quantitative text analysis**

### Structured exercise (1 hour)

**Test set labeling sprint and reliability check (60 min)**

**Part 1 — Labeling sprint (30 min)**
Each group works in pairs to label 20–30 items for their test set. Use your codebook definitions. Label independently — no discussion while labeling.

**Part 2 — Agreement calculation (15 min)**
Compare labels. Calculate a simple percent agreement for each category. Discuss any items where you disagreed:
- Is the item genuinely ambiguous?
- Does the disagreement point to a gap in the codebook?
- Revise definitions where needed.

**Part 3 — Quantitative analysis check-in (15 min)**
TA-facilitated round: each group states what quantitative analysis they are running, what result they have so far (even if preliminary), and what interpretive claim they are building toward. Are the qual and quant analyses speaking to each other yet?

**→ 11:00–13:00: Project work**
Continue quantitative text analysis. Begin drafting the integration between qualitative and quantitative findings.

## Session 13 — Tuesday 26/5
**Topic: Mixed method analysis design and reporting**
*(Following Lecture 8)*

### Structured exercise (1 hour)

**Storyboarding the mixed method analysis (60 min)**

This session asks groups to step back and design their full analysis narrative before writing it up.

**Part 1 — Storyboard drawing (25 min)**
Each group draws their mixed method analysis as a flow diagram on paper or a shared whiteboard. The diagram must show:
- What the qualitative analysis does and what it finds
- What the quantitative analysis does and what it finds
- The *integration point*: where and how do qual and quant findings speak to each other?
- What the combined finding or argument is

**Part 2 — Gallery walk and feedback (20 min)**
Groups post or display their storyboards. Other groups rotate and leave written sticky-note feedback (or comments on a shared doc):
- Is the integration logic clear?
- Does the qual sub-study actually *add* something the quant cannot do alone, or vice versa?
- Is there a clear answer to the research question in the design?

**Part 3 — Revision (15 min)**
Groups revise their storyboard based on feedback. The final storyboard becomes the blueprint for their written assignment structure.

**→ 11:00–13:00: Project work**
Begin or continue writing up the analysis. Clarify the mixed method integration in text. Prepare for the Supervision 6 presentation (28/5).

## Session 14 — Thursday 28/5
**Supervision 6: Milestone 4 — Preliminary final analyses**

### Structured exercise (1 hour)

**Group presentations with structured peer feedback (60 min)**

Each group presents a 7-slide slideshow of their preliminary analysis and receives feedback from peers and the TA.

**Presentation structure (7 slides, ~5 min per group):**
1. Research question and motivation
2. Datasite and topic description
3. Data design
4. Qualitative analysis and results
5. Quantitative analysis and results
6. Integration and combined argument
7. Conclusion and next steps

**Feedback format (5 min per group):**
Peers and TA provide structured feedback on:
- Is the research question clear and answerable?
- Is the data/method choice well-justified?
- Is the qualitative analysis substantive and well-grounded?
- Is the quantitative measure valid?
- Does the integration between qual and quant actually work?

**TA role:** Moderate the feedback. Ensure all groups receive actionable comments. In the final 10 minutes, give overarching feedback to the class on common strengths and gaps.

**→ 11:00–13:00: Project work**
Incorporate feedback into the final written assignment. Plan remaining writing and analysis tasks before the hand-in on 12/6.
