# Chapter 2: From Digital Topics to Mixed-Methods Research Design

## 1. Digital abundance and partial visibility

One of the first things students notice when they begin a digital research project is that there is almost too much to work with. Wherever you point your attention online, you find human activity at scale. Political debates unfold across millions of posts. Support communities for every conceivable life situation have formed on platforms that did not exist fifteen years ago. People narrate their relationships, their grievances, their moral dilemmas, their identities, and their fears in public forums that can be collected, stored, and analyzed. For a social scientist who grew up doing research with surveys and interviews—painstakingly designed instruments that yield modest numbers of responses—the digital landscape can feel overwhelming in the best possible way. Almost everything interesting seems to be happening online, and much of it seems to be leaving traces.

This abundance is real, and it matters. Many of the phenomena that social scientists care most about—political communication and mobilization, the formation of collective identities, the organization of support and solidarity, the dynamics of moral judgment and social norm enforcement, the diffusion of information and misinformation—are now accessible through digital traces in ways that were simply impossible a generation ago. Carlsen and colleagues, working on Danish Facebook groups created around refugee solidarity, were able to collect hundreds of thousands of posts and comments from over a hundred groups, tracking how frames of mobilization shifted in response to political events over a three-year period (Carlsen, Toubøl, and Ralund 2021). That kind of empirical access to the internal life of social movements—at scale, over time, across a large number of organizations—had no realistic precedent in pre-digital social science.

But the abundance comes with a complication that is just as important, and that beginning researchers are slower to notice. The key formulation is this: *everything is online, but only partially*.

What this means is that digital platforms do not provide full or transparent access to the social phenomena they host. They provide selective, mediated, platform-specific access to particular expressions, interactions, discourses, timings, and settings. Political mobilization happens on Facebook—but so does much else; and what appears as political mobilization on Facebook may differ substantially from what the same actors do and say in organizational meetings, in private conversations, or in their actual voting behavior. Moral discourse happens on Reddit—but the AITA forum has specific conventions, a particular demographic composition, and a voting mechanism that shapes what gets posted and how it gets evaluated; it is not a neutral window onto moral life in general. Support practices appear in online communities—but users who post seeking support may differ systematically from those who do not, and the kind of support that works in text-based asynchronous communication differs from what works face to face.

The concept of the *data situation* is useful here (Carlsen et al., forthcoming; Marres 2017). Every digital data source is generated within a specific social and technical situation: a platform with particular affordances and constraints, a community with particular norms and practices, an algorithmic infrastructure that shapes what is visible and what is not. Understanding what a given dataset actually records requires understanding that situation. The digital researcher's task is therefore not simply to find a phenomenon online, but to ask: which aspect of this phenomenon becomes visible through these particular traces, and in what form?

This question has practical consequences. It means that the choice of where and how to collect data is already a theoretically significant decision, not a merely technical one. And it means that digital research is never as simple as pointing a data collection tool at a platform and harvesting what comes out. Before that, the researcher has to understand what kind of thing the data is—what social process generated it, under what conditions, and with what systematic distortions. That understanding is achieved through qualitative engagement with the research site, as we will see in later chapters. For now, the key point is that digital abundance creates genuine empirical opportunity, but it does not replace analytical discipline. The researcher who knows how to turn digital abundance into focused inquiry is in a powerful position. The one who treats abundance as an answer to the question of what to study will quickly become lost.

## 2. From broad themes to researchable topics

If abundance is the starting condition, topic formation is the first act of analytical discipline. A topic is not simply a phenomenon that interests you. It is, as one way of putting it, a device for concentrating your attention on some things and not others (Abbott 2004). And in digital research, where so many things are potentially accessible, the work of choosing what to concentrate on is especially important.

A helpful starting point is to recognize that almost any broad theme can be framed in multiple ways. Take political mobilization. As a broad theme, it is enormous: it encompasses everything from electoral campaigns to street protests, from online petitioning to armed resistance. But the same theme can be reformulated as a specific *event* (a particular protest campaign following a government decision), as a *group* (a grassroots solidarity organization on Facebook), as an *activity* (the practice of liking and sharing activist content), as a *discourse* (the rhetorical frames through which a movement justifies its goals), as a *network* (the relational ties between actors in a movement ecosystem), as a *process* (the trajectory of mobilization from formation to decline), as a *technology* (the role of a specific platform in coordinating action), or as a *location* (the regional distribution of activist groups). Each framing produces a different research object and opens a different line of inquiry. Each is also differentially accessible in digital data: networks leave relational traces; discourses leave textual ones; activities leave behavioral ones; processes leave temporal ones.

This flexibility is one of the genuine analytical gifts of digital research. Because online archives typically encode the same phenomenon in several ways at once—a Facebook group contains relational data (who is connected to whom), textual data (what people write), behavioral data (who posts when), and temporal data (how activity changes over time)—a researcher who has assembled a digital dataset often has options about which angle to pursue that a researcher working with a single-mode data source does not. The question is which framing is most productive given the theoretical interests at stake.

The movement from broad theme to specific framing requires what qualitative researchers call *casing*: the work of deciding what a given empirical object is a case of (Ragin 2009). This is one of the most important—and most underappreciated—analytical moves in digital research. Suppose you begin with an empirical observation: a cluster of Facebook groups formed by Danish volunteers during the 2015 refugee crisis, offering practical support to newly arrived refugees. What is this a case of? It could be a case of humanitarian volunteering; of civil society mobilization; of social movement activity; of non-contentious collective action; of digital solidarity practices; of welfare state governance from below. Each casing connects the empirical object to a different body of literature, generates different theoretical expectations, and makes different aspects of the data analytically significant.

The direction can also run the other way. A researcher with an established theoretical interest—say, in how collective action frames compete within social movements—might begin not with an empirical object but with a *case selection* question: given what I want to study, what would be a good empirical case? Case selection is not simply a matter of finding any case that fits the theoretical category. It is an analytical judgment about which case would allow the theoretical question to be studied well, and under what conditions. Digital cases are particularly interesting here because digitization does not leave the phenomenon unchanged. Online mobilization is not simply an online version of mobilization; it may operate through different mechanisms, at different speeds, with different constraints on participation and different affordances for organization. Choosing a digital case for a theory developed on non-digital data therefore requires active consideration of whether and how the digital context alters the phenomenon (Marres 2017; Venturini et al. 2018).

Concretely, when a group of students in this course decided to study extremist ideology through the lens of incel culture on Reddit, they were performing a casing operation: taking a specific online community and asking what broader theoretical category it instantiated. Their research question—what tenets of incel culture resemble the structure of extremist ideology?—located an empirical object within an established theoretical framework, connecting a digital phenomenon to a well-developed body of research on radicalization and ideological structure. Their analytical payoff came precisely from the gap between what the theory expected and what the data showed. That gap requires casing to be visible at all.

## 3. From topic to question

Once you have a topic and a framing, you need a question. This sounds obvious, but getting the question right is harder than it appears, and it matters more than students often realize. A poorly formulated question does not simply yield bad answers; it prevents learning from happening in the first place.

Four basic rules help here. First, a good research question should be *answerable*: there should be some form of evidence, in principle available, that could resolve it. Questions that cannot be answered by any conceivable evidence are not research questions but philosophical positions. In digital research, this means asking whether the data you plan to collect could actually speak to the question you are asking—a discipline that is more demanding than it sounds, given how easy it is to collect data that is merely related to a question without being able to answer it.

Second, a good question should produce *learning*: answering it should tell you something you did not already know. This rules out questions that are so carefully hedged, or so aligned with existing theory, that any plausible finding would simply confirm what was already expected. Research is useful precisely when it can be surprised. Questions should be formulated so that there exist at least two possible answers, one of which would be genuinely unexpected and theoretically significant.

Third, a good question should *not assume its answer in advance*. This sounds like a restatement of the previous point, but it targets something slightly different: the tendency to formulate questions in normatively loaded or presupposing ways. A question like "why is online political debate so toxic?" already assumes that it is, and forecloses the possibility that the data might reveal something more nuanced. Questions should be formulated in a way that genuinely opens the phenomenon rather than foreclosing it.

Fourth, and especially important in digital research, a good question is *iterative*. The first formulation is rarely the final one. Digital data are often unstructured, only partially understood before collection, and capable of generating surprises that redirect the inquiry. It is normal and legitimate to revise a research question as you learn more about your empirical material—as long as the revision is transparent and motivated by what you have learned rather than by the desire to match a result you happened to find. The process of moving between data and question is one of the defining features of qualitative inquiry, and it is fully applicable in digital research.

There is a fifth rule that is specific to mixed-methods research: a good mixed-methods question is one for which neither qualitative nor quantitative evidence alone would be fully satisfactory. If your question can be answered entirely through close reading of a handful of documents, there is no need for computational classification or statistical analysis; use qualitative methods and use them well. If your question can be answered entirely by counting patterns in a large corpus, there is no need for deep interpretive engagement; use the counting. The justification for a mixed approach comes from the structure of the question itself: from the fact that interpretive and distributional evidence are both necessary, and that each constrains and enriches the other. Asking "how do Ukrainian military brigades use social media to mobilize support?" requires qualitative analysis to understand what kinds of appeals are being made and why they resonate—and quantitative analysis to assess how broadly those appeals distribute across posts, audiences, and time. Neither alone is enough. Together, they answer the question.

## 4. Types of questions

Research questions in social science vary along two important dimensions that are worth distinguishing clearly, because confusing them leads to unnecessary methodological debates.

The first dimension distinguishes *theory-testing* from *theory-generating* questions. Theory-testing questions begin with an established theoretical proposition and ask whether it holds in a new empirical setting or under new conditions. Theory-generating questions begin with an empirical puzzle and ask what theoretical account best explains it. These are not the same thing, and treating them as equivalent creates problems: a theory-testing framework will systematically miss discoveries that lie outside the tested hypothesis; a theory-generating framework will systematically struggle to accumulate knowledge by confronting established expectations.

The second dimension distinguishes *descriptive* from *causal* questions. Descriptive questions ask what something is, how it is distributed, or how it has changed. Causal questions ask why: what produces what, under what conditions, through what mechanisms. These too are genuinely different challenges, requiring different evidence and different analytical strategies.

Importantly, these two dimensions are independent. Combining them yields four distinct types of questions (Figure 2.1), each legitimate and each calling for different analytical approaches. A theory-testing descriptive question asks whether an expected pattern actually exists in the data. A theory-generating descriptive question asks what patterns are present in the data before any theory has been imposed. A theory-testing causal question asks whether a hypothesized mechanism operates as expected. A theory-generating causal question asks what explains a surprising pattern, and works toward a new theoretical account.

> **Figure 2.1.** A typology of research questions across two dimensions: theory-testing versus theory-generating (rows) and descriptive versus causal (columns). The four resulting cells are all legitimate forms of social science inquiry, and qualitative and quantitative methods can contribute to all four.

One practical implication of this typology is that qualitative analysis is not restricted to the theory-generating, descriptive corner of the matrix where it is often implicitly located. Qualitative analysis can support theory testing (by providing evidence that a proposed mechanism is operating as expected in a specific case) and causal reasoning (by tracing the process through which an outcome is produced). Similarly, quantitative analysis is not restricted to confirmatory or causal work; exploratory and descriptive uses of large-scale data are among the most valuable contributions of the computational turn in social science.

A second practical resource for question formulation is a set of interrogative heuristics: *what*, *how*, *where*, *why*, *who*, and *when*. What questions ask about the character and composition of a phenomenon: what are the defining features of this discourse, this identity, this community? How questions ask about processes and mechanisms: how does solidarity get organized, how does radicalization unfold, how do frames compete? Where questions ask about setting and context: where on this platform does this activity concentrate, and how does the setting shape what occurs? Why questions ask about causes and motivations: why do some movement organizations become contentious while others do not? Who questions focus on actors: who participates, who speaks, who is excluded? When questions attend to timing and sequence: when do political events trigger online mobilization, and how quickly does it decay?

Digital data are particularly productive for these heuristics because a well-assembled digital archive often allows the researcher to address several of these dimensions from within the same dataset. The same archive of Facebook posts can yield *what* (the content and framing of posts), *who* (which actors are active), *when* (temporal patterns of activity), *where* (which groups or settings are involved), and *how* (the interactional dynamics through which outcomes are produced). This is one of the genuine analytical advantages of digital research: the integration of multiple analytical angles within a single data source.

## 5. How digital mixed methods expand the questions we can ask

Digital data have historically been approached through two broad theoretical strategies. The first treats digital data as *new data for testing old theories*: an opportunity to bring established theoretical frameworks to bear on phenomena that were previously difficult to study at scale. The second treats digital data as a basis for *theory generation*: the patterns visible in large corpora can challenge existing assumptions, suggest new categories, and prompt the development of theoretical accounts that would not have been visible through other means.

Both strategies are important, and both face characteristic difficulties when pursued alone.

Theory testing with digital data inherits all the standard challenges of empirical testing, plus some new ones. The most important is that digital data are generated within specific social and technical situations that may not match the scope conditions of the theory being tested. A theory about the dynamics of political participation, developed on survey data from national populations, may or may not apply to the self-selected, platform-shaped populations that appear in social media data. Applying the theory without investigating whether those scope conditions are met risks producing misleading results: apparent confirmations that reflect the data's selection properties rather than the theory's validity, or apparent disconfirmations that reflect platform specificity rather than theoretical failure.

Theory generation with digital data faces a complementary problem. Computational methods for pattern detection—topic models, clustering algorithms, keyword analyses—can reveal structures in large corpora that no human reader could have detected manually. But detected patterns are not the same as theoretically meaningful categories. The question of whether a computationally identified pattern corresponds to something socially real, and what it means in the context where it was produced, requires the kind of qualitative grounding that computational methods alone cannot provide. As Carlsen and Ralund (2022) have argued, the assumption that machine-detected patterns automatically constitute valid social science categories is one of the central methodological weaknesses of the computational grounded theory framework in its dominant form.

Mixed digital methods strengthen both strategies by linking interpretive and distributional evidence in a systematic way. For theory testing, mixed methods help researchers assess whether the phenomenon being studied is actually the phenomenon the theory is about—whether the digital instantiation matches the theoretical concept well enough for the test to be meaningful. For theory generation, mixed methods help validate whether computationally detected patterns are socially meaningful, by anchoring quantitative discoveries in qualitative understanding of the context in which they occurred.

An important practical heuristic follows from this: when beginning a digital research project, it is worth actively looking for *mono-method literatures* and asking what they may be missing. A literature built on survey data typically knows a lot about individual-level variation but relatively little about the social contexts in which individuals are embedded. A literature built on ethnographic fieldwork in single sites typically knows a lot about specific local dynamics but relatively little about how those dynamics distribute across a larger population. Digital mixed methods can challenge both kinds of limits: introducing contextual evidence where statistical research has proceeded without it, and introducing distributional evidence where ethnographic research has generalized cautiously from small samples. In each case, the payoff comes not simply from adding methods but from asking what the existing literature cannot see, and designing a study that sees it (Carlsen, Toubøl, and Ralund 2021).

## 6. From question to design

A research design is not a plan for collecting data. It is a structure for answering a question. The core challenge in design is alignment: making sure that the question, the data, and the analytical strategy are matched to one another in a way that allows the question to be answered convincingly.

In mixed-methods research, two broad motivations govern how different analytical components relate to one another. *Confirmatory designs* use different methods to check or challenge one another: the same substantive claim is supported by evidence of multiple kinds, and the convergence or divergence of that evidence is itself analytically significant. A classic example is combining survey-based measures of hiring discrimination with audit study measures of actual discriminatory behavior: if they agree, the finding is more robust; if they diverge, the divergence is itself a discovery. *Complementary designs* use different methods to address different aspects of the same research question: qualitative analysis establishes the meaning and mechanism of a phenomenon, while quantitative analysis establishes its scope and distribution. Neither alone would fully answer the question; together they provide a rounded answer.

Designs can also be organized sequentially or concurrently. In *concurrent designs*, qualitative and quantitative components run in parallel and are integrated at the interpretation stage. In *sequential designs*, one component builds on the other: typically, qualitative analysis precedes and shapes quantitative analysis, with insights from immersive engagement with the data guiding the design of computational measures and the interpretation of statistical results. Sequential designs are especially natural in digital research, because the interpretive work required to understand what a digital archive actually records typically needs to happen before meaningful quantitative analysis can be designed.

This brings us to what is perhaps the most important structural feature of digital mixed-methods research: it is typically more *iterative and modular* than classical mixed-methods research, which often envisions a small number of large sub-studies (a survey and a set of interviews; an experiment and an ethnography). In digital research, once an archive has been assembled, researchers often perform many smaller mixed-methods operations within it—moving back and forth between close reading and pattern analysis, between qualitative category development and computational classification, between individual cases and population-level distributions. Each operation is relatively contained; together, they accumulate into a comprehensive analysis.

A typical sequence for digital mixed-methods research looks something like Figure 2.2: a researcher first evaluates and understands a potential research site through immersive observation; collects or samples data from that site; uses computational tools to assist reading and support the selection of documents for qualitative analysis; performs focused qualitative analysis to develop and ground analytical categories; quantifies analytically important features through supervised classification; and integrates qualitative and quantitative results in a joint interpretation.

> **Figure 2.2.** The sequential analysis design typical in digital mixed-methods research. The process moves from immersive site evaluation through data collection, computer-assisted discovery, qualitative analysis, classification and measurement, and joint interpretation. Arrows indicate the sequential dependencies between stages, with qualitative understanding informing each subsequent quantitative operation.

This design has several properties worth noting. It is sequential in the sense that each stage depends on the previous one: the coding categories developed through qualitative analysis are the basis for the computational classifier; the classifier's output is interpreted in light of the qualitative understanding developed earlier. It is iterative in the sense that surprises at any stage can send the researcher back to an earlier one: a distributional finding that does not fit the qualitative account may prompt a revision of the analytical categories, or a return to the archive for closer reading. And it is integrated in a strong sense: the qualitative and quantitative components are not parallel studies that are compared at the end, but sequential operations on the same data, each shaping the next.

This design structure also shapes what counts as evidence and how findings should be reported. A finding in digital mixed-methods research is not simply a statistical result or simply a qualitative interpretation. It is the product of an integrated process in which interpretation and distribution have been mutually disciplined. The validity of the finding depends on the quality of each stage—on how well the site was understood before data were collected, how carefully categories were developed through qualitative analysis, how rigorously the classifier was validated, and how transparently the joint interpretation integrates the evidence. Later chapters will address each of these stages in detail.

The takeaway for this chapter is simpler: design follows from question, and question follows from the analytical work of framing a topic, identifying what aspect of it is visible in available digital data, and specifying what kind of evidence—interpretive, distributional, or both—would constitute a convincing answer. Digital abundance provides the raw material. Mixed-methods design provides the structure for turning that raw material into knowledge.
