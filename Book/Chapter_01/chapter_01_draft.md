# Chapter 1: Something Is Mixed Up in the Social Sciences

---

## 1. The new data situation: something is mixed up

On Reddit's r/AmITheAsshole forum, people post accounts of morally charged situations from their everyday lives and ask strangers to pass judgment. The format is deceptively simple. A user narrates a situation—a dispute with a sibling over an inheritance, a confrontation with a colleague about a shared workspace, a difficult choice made under pressure from a partner—and ends with a direct question: "Am I the asshole?" Other users respond with one of a handful of standardized verdicts: "NTA" (not the asshole), "YTA" (you're the asshole), "ESH" (everyone sucks here), "NAH" (no assholes here). The post that accumulates the most votes in a given direction determines the community's official verdict. The forum has millions of members and generates thousands of posts every day.

From one angle, this is exactly the kind of data that has excited social scientists over the past two decades. It is large in scale, continuously produced, and available for analysis. People share detailed narratives about their moral lives—their values, their relationships, their reasoning about what is fair and what is not. For anyone interested in moral norms, social judgment, or the communication of ethical reasoning, the forum is an extraordinary empirical resource. It offers something that was simply unavailable to researchers a generation ago: a mass archive of situated moral experience, produced at the population level, accessible without a survey.

From another angle, what actually happens in this forum is extraordinarily complex. When someone posts to r/AmITheAsshole, they are not simply reporting facts. They are composing a narrative with a particular rhetorical purpose: to invite sympathy, to secure validation, or sometimes to genuinely work through moral uncertainty. The format has its own conventions. Regular users know that posts written from a certain stance—self-aware, generous to the other party, rich with contextual detail—tend to receive more charitable verdicts. The community has developed elaborate meta-norms about what kinds of situations qualify for genuine moral judgment and which ones reveal the poster as having already made up their mind. A verdict of "NTA" is not simply a moral assessment. It is a communicative act within a specific social genre, calibrated to the conventions of the platform, and meaningful only in relation to the full context of the post it addresses.

Researchers have analyzed this material computationally. Botzer and colleagues (2022), for instance, collected tens of thousands of AITA posts and trained machine learning models to predict the community's moral verdicts from the textual features of the posts. Their models achieve reasonable predictive accuracy. The general enterprise is technically admirable: it demonstrates that something systematic is happening in how the community evaluates moral situations, and that this systematicity is legible, at least partially, to a machine. But consider what the analysis leaves aside. To predict whether a post will receive a "YTA" verdict, the model treats language as a statistical surface—patterns of words, sentence structures, sentiment signals—without asking what the words mean to the people who wrote them, or how meaning is shaped by the forum's social conventions. The analysis answers a distributional question (what predicts the verdict?) with some success, while remaining agnostic about the interpretive questions that any serious understanding of moral life would require. What kinds of situations does this community consider genuinely blameworthy, and why? How does the forum's genre—its specific prompt format, its voting mechanism, its cultural norms—shape what gets expressed and how it gets evaluated? What does it mean, socially and morally, to seek validation from millions of anonymous strangers? These are questions about meaning, about context, about what the data is actually a record of. And they are not answered by predicting verdicts from word patterns.

This is not a criticism of any particular study. It is a description of a general condition in digital social research. Researchers are increasingly working with data that is qualitative in several of the most important traditional senses of that word: context-dependent, open-ended, produced through situated social practices, saturated with meaning that cannot be read directly from its surface (Carlsen et al., forthcoming). At the same time, the scale of such data makes purely manual, interpretive analysis impractical. And many of the questions researchers want to ask—about prevalence, variation across settings, change over time, the association between one social phenomenon and another—are distributional in character. They require systematic analysis of many observations. The result is a research situation in which interpretive and distributional challenges are deeply entangled. You cannot answer the distributional questions without first understanding what the data actually records—and that understanding is irreducibly qualitative. But you cannot develop that understanding by reading everything manually at the scale at which contemporary digital data typically arrives.

This is what I mean when I say that something is mixed up in the social sciences. The mixing is not primarily a matter of researchers choosing to combine methods because a broader toolkit seems useful. It is a structural feature of the research situation itself. The data, the questions, and the analytical challenges are already mixed, in the sense that qualitative and quantitative elements are woven together from the outset. Quantitative analysis of moral judgment on Reddit without qualitative understanding of what moral judgment means in that setting is likely to produce misleading results. Qualitative analysis alone, without systematic attention to distribution and variation, leaves important questions unanswered. Neither approach, pursued in isolation, is adequate to the object.

The phrase "something is mixed up" is therefore both descriptive and provocative. Descriptively, it names a real feature of the contemporary research landscape: our data, our analytical challenges, and our methods no longer sort cleanly into the categories that older methodological traditions bequeathed to us. Provocatively, it suggests that this condition requires a response—a new way of thinking about what it takes to do social research in the digital age. The rest of this chapter tries to explain how we arrived at this situation, and what a genuine response would require.

## 2. How we got here: digital methods and computational social science

To understand why social research finds itself in this mixed-up condition, it helps to consider the two major traditions that have shaped the field's response to the digital transformation of social life. Both traditions recognized that something important had changed. But each diagnosed the change differently, and each left something essential unresolved.

The first tradition goes under various names—digital methods, web science, internet research—but its most distinctive methodological formulation comes from Richard Rogers and the work associated with the Digital Methods Initiative in Amsterdam. The central claim of this tradition is that the right response to the emergence of digital social life is not to apply old methods to new data sources, but to develop methods that are native to digital environments. In *Digital Methods* (2013), Rogers drew a sharp distinction between "methods of the internet" and "methods on the internet." The former take the specific objects, structures, and affordances of digital platforms as the starting point for inquiry. The latter simply use the internet as a convenient source of data for analyses designed without regard to its specific character.

What does it mean to take digital objects seriously on their own terms? The hyperlink is Rogers' canonical example, and it is instructive. A hyperlink is not simply equivalent to a social tie in the ordinary sociological sense. It is a specific digital relation with its own technical properties, its own cultural meanings, and its own place within the web's architecture of attention and authority. When one website links to another, it makes a claim, performs an endorsement, establishes a form of categorization—while also shaping how information circulates and how some voices become authoritative while others remain marginal. To analyze link structures using frameworks designed for, say, friendship networks or citation graphs is to risk importing assumptions that distort what is actually happening. The digital methods tradition insisted that researchers must first ask what kind of thing a hyperlink actually is before deploying analytical frameworks designed for something else.

This methodological sensibility has a clear virtue. It keeps researchers attentive to the ways that digital platforms actively shape the social life they mediate. Social media platforms do not simply provide a stage on which pre-existing social relations are displayed. As Noortje Marres (2017) has argued, they are active participants in the constitution of social life: their interfaces, algorithms, affordances, and user norms shape what can be said, who gets heard, and how meaning is made. To analyze social media data without attending to this mediation is to risk studying the platform's effects while treating them as properties of the society itself. The digital methods tradition put mediation—the specific, technically and culturally shaped ways in which digital environments structure communication—at the center of analysis.

The second tradition, computational social science, follows a rather different logic. Its origins lie not in media studies or internet research but in the quantitative social sciences and in the computational tools developed within computer science and statistics. Its defining moment was the recognition, across the mid-2000s, that digital platforms were generating data at a scale and granularity that simply had not existed before. Lazer and colleagues' programmatic essay in *Science* (2009) was an early landmark, arguing that the availability of digital trace data represented a potential revolution for the social sciences. Salganik's *Bit by Bit: Social Research in the Digital Age* (2017) offered the most comprehensive and careful account of what this revolution offers and requires—and became, rightly, the field's most widely read methodological text.

The logic of this tradition is essentially one of opportunity and extension. The analytical frameworks of quantitative social science—network analysis, regression modeling, text classification, experimental design—could now be applied to data sources of unprecedented size and behavioral granularity. Where a researcher studying political communication once worked with hundreds of survey respondents, it was now possible to analyze millions of posts. Where a network researcher had worked with small samples of interview-elicited ties, it was now possible to reconstruct interaction structures from billions of digital traces. Salganik captures the spirit of the tradition well when he notes that digital data allows researchers to study social life with a completeness, continuity, and granularity that was previously impossible (Salganik 2017). The underlying methodological logic remained broadly continuous with established quantitative social science: digital data expanded the empirical field and opened new analytical possibilities, but it did not necessarily require a fundamental rethinking of the methods themselves.

Both traditions contributed something essential. The computational social science tradition demonstrated that social science could operate at genuinely new scales, and that doing so opened analytical possibilities that were simply unavailable before. The ability to study variation across large and heterogeneous populations, to detect rare events, to trace the diffusion of information, to identify the structural features of large social networks—these are real advances, and they have produced genuine knowledge (Lazer et al. 2021). The digital methods tradition, meanwhile, kept alive the crucial question of what digital data actually is: what kinds of social processes and meaning-making practices it captures, and what it systematically obscures. Its insistence on attending to the specificity of the digital object prevented the naive assumption that digital traces are simply transparent records of pre-existing social facts that can be analyzed with any available tool.

There is, however, a telling asymmetry between the two traditions. The computational social science tradition, at least in its dominant formulations, treated the digital transformation primarily as a *data event*. The interesting thing about the internet was the data it generated. The question was how to exploit that data most effectively using the best available analytical tools. The digital methods tradition treated the transformation as a *methodological and theoretical event*. The interesting thing about the internet was that it instantiated new kinds of social objects, new forms of sociality, and new relations between media and society—and that understanding these required genuine innovation in both method and theory.

What neither tradition fully made central, however, was the specific methodological condition that now defines much of digital social research: the situation in which interpretive and distributional challenges must be handled together, not sequentially, not by different research communities, but within a single integrated research process.

## 3. What both traditions left unresolved

The limitation that motivates this book is not hard to see once you have a concrete data situation in front of you. Return to the AITA example. A researcher in the computational social science tradition might approach this forum primarily as a source of data about moral judgment at scale: an opportunity to study what factors predict moral verdicts across a very large sample of cases, to identify which features of a situation systematically elicit blame, and to test hypotheses drawn from moral psychology or moral sociology. A researcher in the digital methods tradition might focus on the forum's specific affordances and communicative genres: asking what kind of social practice the AITA format instantiates, how the community's norms shape what gets posted and how it gets evaluated, and what this tells us about the digitally mediated constitution of moral community. Both are valuable analytical moves. But neither, pursued in isolation, is adequate to the full challenge.

The computational approach, pursued without qualitative grounding, risks studying the statistical surface of the data while remaining agnostic about what that surface records. If you do not understand what moral judgment means in the AITA community—how members distinguish between situations that warrant blame and those that do not, what rhetorical conventions shape the framing of posts, how the voting mechanism interacts with community norms to produce verdicts—you cannot be confident that your quantitative measure of moral judgment actually measures what you think it measures. The interpretive work is not supplementary to the quantitative analysis. It is constitutive of it. As Carlsen and Ralund (2022) have argued in the context of computational text analysis more broadly, skipping the phase of qualitative immersion and grounding does not produce a more objective analysis; it produces an analysis whose validity cannot be assessed, because the relationship between the formal measure and the social phenomenon it is supposed to capture has never been established.

The digital methods approach, meanwhile, offers sophisticated tools for understanding the platform's affordances and its native objects. It is well-suited to answering the question of what kind of thing the AITA forum is. But it has not developed a general methodology for moving from qualitative understanding of what a digital object is to systematic, reproducible analysis of large-scale patterns. The question of how to study distribution and variation—how prevalent is a given type of moral reasoning, does it vary by the type of situation described, or by the demographic characteristics of the community?—is not well answered by attending to platform affordances, however carefully.

What digital social research characteristically requires is the capacity to handle two forms of uncertainty at the same time. There is, first, *interpretive uncertainty*: the challenge of understanding what digital data actually records, what situated practices and meaning-making conventions shaped its production, what ambiguities and multiple readings it supports, and what theoretical framework is adequate to the phenomenon it captures. This is the kind of uncertainty that qualitative researchers have developed sophisticated tools for handling—ethnographic immersion, close reading, theoretical sampling, constant comparison, abductive reasoning. Second, there is *distributional uncertainty*: the challenge of determining how prevalent a given pattern is, whether it varies across populations or settings, how it has changed over time, and whether an apparent association between two variables reflects something real or is an artifact of sampling and measurement. This is the kind of uncertainty that quantitative and computational researchers have developed tools for—sampling theory, statistical inference, model comparison, replication.

The point is that these two forms of uncertainty, though genuinely different in character, are interdependent in practice. Interpretive uncertainty cannot be resolved by accumulating more data; distributional uncertainty cannot be resolved by reading more carefully. But neither can they be resolved independently of one another. You need qualitative understanding to build valid quantitative measures: to know that what you are counting actually corresponds to the social phenomenon you are interested in. And you need quantitative analysis to determine whether the patterns you have identified through close reading reflect something general or are idiosyncrasies of the specific cases you happened to examine. The resolution of one form of uncertainty shapes the resolution of the other.

This interdependence has implications for how we think about research design. It is tempting to imagine a clean division of labor: qualitative researchers handle interpretive uncertainty; quantitative researchers handle distributional uncertainty; the two teams collaborate. This division can work, but it often does not, because the iterative feedback between interpretive and distributional analysis is difficult to achieve across a disciplinary or methodological divide. The qualitative researcher who has spent months immersed in the field develops insights that are difficult to translate into the formal categories required for quantitative analysis. The quantitative researcher who identifies a surprising distributional pattern needs to go back to the qualitative material to understand what the pattern means. In practice, the integration must happen within the research process itself, not just between research teams.

It is also important to clarify what this challenge is not. It is not a general argument for "using both qualitative and quantitative methods," in the loose sense in which that phrase is sometimes invoked. The issue is not method plurality per se. It is methodological integration: developing a research strategy in which interpretive and distributional challenges are addressed in a coordinated way, where each phase of the research genuinely informs the others, and where the outputs of qualitative analysis shape the design of quantitative measures while quantitative patterns direct further qualitative investigation (Blok, Bang Carlsen, & Albris 2022; Small 2011). This kind of integration is harder to achieve than simple method combination, and it requires more than goodwill toward both traditions. It requires what one might call a *methodology*—a set of principles, procedures, and criteria—that is genuinely adequate to the mixed-up condition of digital social research.

The growing recognition of this need is visible across the field, even where its methodological implications have not been fully drawn out. Grigoropoulou and Small (2022) have argued in *Nature Human Behaviour* that the data revolution in social science needs qualitative research—not as a supplement or afterthought, but as an integral component of how digital data is understood, sampled, interpreted, and validated. Carlsen and colleagues have made the case that digital trace data is qualitative data in a deep and consequential sense: context-dependent, meaning-saturated, and requiring interpretive work before it can be scientifically productive, regardless of its scale (Carlsen et al., forthcoming). These arguments point in the same direction. They suggest that the excitement about scale and computational power has, in at least some quarters, outpaced the methodological thinking about what it actually takes to analyze meaning-rich digital data well. What looks like a data revolution is, from another angle, a methodological challenge—and one that is distinctively mixed.

## 4. The task of this course and book

This book is an attempt to develop a research strategy adequate to the mixed-up condition of digital social research. Its ambition is not to advocate for a general pluralism of methods—that argument has been made effectively elsewhere (Small 2011). Its ambition is more specific: to show how researchers can work productively and rigorously in a setting where the data, the questions, and the research objects are already mixed, and where interpretive and distributional challenges are intertwined from the start.

The approach developed in these pages is built around the characteristic situation of contemporary digital social research: a researcher who has access to large-scale digital data—posts, comments, threads, interactions, images—and who wants to use that data to understand something meaningful about social life. This researcher faces the full challenge described above. She needs to understand what her data actually records, which requires qualitative engagement with its production context and meaning structures. She needs to develop analytical categories that are valid—that genuinely capture the social phenomena she is interested in—which requires a process of empirical grounding that cannot be shortcut by computational convenience. And she needs to analyze distributions and patterns in ways that support systematic inference beyond the specific cases she has read closely. The research strategy in this book addresses all three challenges in an integrated way, and takes seriously their interdependence.

A few things about this strategy are worth flagging from the outset. First, it is genuinely sequential and iterative rather than simply parallel. The tradition of mixed methods in the social sciences often envisions qualitative and quantitative work running alongside one another, each providing a complementary perspective. The approach here is different: qualitative analysis comes first, not because quantitative analysis is less important, but because qualitative understanding is a precondition for valid quantitative measurement. At the same time, quantitative results regularly send researchers back to qualitative material to understand patterns that were unexpected or anomalous. The process is a spiral, not a two-lane highway.

Second, the strategy takes the digital platform and its affordances seriously—in the spirit of the digital methods tradition—while also asking systematic questions about distribution and variation, in the spirit of computational social science. It tries to inherit the best from both traditions while avoiding the limitations of each pursued alone.

Third, this is a book about method, not about any particular substantive topic. The examples drawn upon throughout—refugee solidarity movements on Facebook, moral discourse on Reddit, political mobilization in online communities, desk-booking patterns in a hybrid workplace—are chosen because they illuminate the methodological challenges, not because they constitute a unified research program. The methods travel; the examples illustrate them.

Each chapter takes up a specific element of the integrated research strategy. The next chapter provides an overview of the full research process and introduces the framework that will guide the rest of the book. Subsequent chapters address in sequence the major stages of a digital mixed methods project: finding and evaluating a research site; collecting data at scale while attending to what one's sample actually represents; understanding the site and its communicative conventions through immersive qualitative engagement; using computational tools to navigate and discover categories in large corpora; building valid measures through a combination of qualitative analysis and supervised classification; and integrating qualitative and quantitative results into a coherent analysis. Throughout, the focus is on the specific challenges that arise when working with digital trace data—challenges that are neither purely qualitative nor purely quantitative, but genuinely mixed in the sense this chapter has tried to establish.

Something is indeed mixed up in the social sciences. The chapters that follow are an attempt to make sense of that condition—and to turn it from a source of confusion into a foundation for better research.
