# Outro Critique — Lecture 13

**Syllabus title:** Outro / closing session
**Current folder:** Lecture 13 (to be renamed Lecture_Outro)

---

## What Works

- The "what is missing" section (visualisations, image analysis, advanced LLMs) is pedagogically mature — it models intellectual humility and explicitly positions the course within a larger methodological landscape.
- The oral exam guidance is clear and practical, especially the reassurance that the oral exam can only improve a grade.
- The "how to write a good results section" guidance (qualitative, quantitative, discussion) is useful and well-structured.

---

## Critiques

1. **The lecture tells students what they have learned rather than asking them to synthesise it.** A brief retrospective exercise — groups write three key methodological lessons, then share — would be more active and help consolidate learning.

2. **"What is missing" undersells current capabilities.** Multimodal LLMs (GPT-4o, Claude, Gemini), CLIP-based image classification, and audio analysis are now mature enough to be presented as current options, not just future directions. Update framing from "what we have not covered" to "where this course fits in a larger landscape."

3. **Ethics of representation is thin.** The ethical reporting section is announced but not developed. Who is harmed by how we report? How do we anonymise or aggregate responsibly? How do we represent marginalised communities without reinforcing stigma? This deserves explicit treatment as a closing theme.

4. **Exam prep content is duplicated from Lecture 12.** The exam "focus", "must haves", and "prototype" sections are near-identical to those in Lecture 12. Keep the oral exam guidance here; remove the written assignment prototype (already covered in full in Lecture 12).

5. **No assigned reading.** The Outro has no reading. While some might argue this is appropriate, a short closing provocation would elevate the session.

---

## Alternative Organisation

Reframe as a genuine closing event:
1. Student synthesis exercise: each group identifies and shares their three most important methodological lessons (15 min)
2. Instructor synthesis: what have we learned together — connect back to the course's opening social puzzle from Lecture 1
3. "What is missing" — current and future methods landscape (10 min)
4. Ethics of representation — the responsible researcher's last mile (10 min)
5. Oral exam preparation (15 min)

---

## Readings

**Add:**
- Markham, Annette. "Fabrication as ethical practice." *Information, Communication & Society* 15.3 (2012): 334–353 (~19 pp.) — ethics of representing online subjects in publications; closes the ethics thread opened in Lecture 3
- D'Ignazio, Catherine & Lauren F. Klein. "Data Feminism." MIT Press (2020), ch. 1 (~20 pp.) — reframes what counts as "good" data science; a stimulating closing provocation that students will remember
